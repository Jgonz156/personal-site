## The Third Perspective

Imagine you're writing software for an aircraft's autopilot system. You can't just "test it and see" — a bug at 35,000 feet is not a learning opportunity. You need to **prove** that the software behaves correctly for *all* possible inputs, not just the ones you tested.

> "Testing shows the presence, not the absence, of bugs." — Edsger W. Dijkstra. This famous observation captures exactly why axiomatic semantics exists. No amount of testing can prove a program correct for all inputs. Only formal proof can provide that guarantee.

This is what axiomatic semantics gives us: the ability to reason about programs using **logic alone**, without ever executing them. The meaning of a program is not what it does (operational) or what function it computes (denotational) — it's **what you can prove about it**.

<DefinitionBox term="Axiomatic Semantics">

**Axiomatic semantics** defines the meaning of a program through logical assertions about its behavior. Programs are annotated with preconditions and postconditions, and inference rules describe how to derive properties of compound programs from properties of their parts.

</DefinitionBox>

| Approach | Question | Implementation |
|----------|----------|----------------|
| Operational | How does it *run*? | Interpreter |
| Denotational | What does it *compute*? | Transpiler |
| **Axiomatic** | **What can we *guarantee*?** | **Static Verifier** |

## Hoare Logic: Programs as Logical Propositions

In 1969, C.A.R. Hoare published a landmark paper that proposed a simple but powerful idea: **annotate programs with logical assertions**, then use inference rules to prove that the annotations are consistent.

> C.A.R. Hoare (later Sir Tony Hoare) also invented Quicksort, CSP (Communicating Sequential Processes), and the concept of null references (which he later called his "billion-dollar mistake"). His 1969 paper "An Axiomatic Basis for Computer Programming" launched the entire field of program verification.

<DefinitionBox term="Hoare Triple">

A **Hoare triple** has the form:

<DisplayMath formula="\{P\} \; C \; \{Q\}" />

where:
- $P$ is the **precondition** — what must be true *before* $C$ executes
- $C$ is a **command** (statement or program)
- $Q$ is the **postcondition** — what is guaranteed to be true *after* $C$ executes

The triple asserts: **if $P$ holds before executing $C$, then $Q$ holds after.**

</DefinitionBox>

For example:

<DisplayMath formula="\{x = 5\} \quad y = x + 1 \quad \{y = 6\}" />

This says: if $x = 5$ before the assignment, then $y = 6$ after. We didn't run the program — we *proved* a property about it by reasoning about what the assignment does.

Here's a more general example:

<DisplayMath formula="\{x > 0\} \quad y = x * 2 \quad \{y > 0 \wedge y = 2x\}" />

This says: for **any** positive $x$, after multiplying by 2, $y$ is positive and equals $2x$. This is a universal statement — it holds for infinitely many inputs, yet we proved it without running a single one.

## The Assignment Axiom

The foundation of Hoare Logic is the **assignment axiom** — and it runs *backward*:

<DefinitionBox term="Assignment Axiom">

The **assignment axiom** states:

<DisplayMath formula="\{Q[x / e]\} \quad x = e \quad \{Q\}" />

To establish postcondition $Q$ after the assignment $x = e$, the precondition must be $Q$ with every occurrence of $x$ replaced by $e$. The notation $Q[x/e]$ means "substitute $e$ for $x$ in $Q$."

</DefinitionBox>

> The backward direction is counterintuitive at first. Most people think forward: "I know $x$ is 5, so after $y = x + 1$, $y$ is 6." The axiom works backward: "I want $y = 6$ after the assignment. What must be true before?" This backward reasoning is the key to mechanical proof construction — we start with the goal and work toward the assumptions.

For example, to prove $\{?\} \; y = x + 1 \; \{y = 6\}$, we substitute $x + 1$ for $y$ in the postcondition: $y = 6$ becomes $x + 1 = 6$, which simplifies to $x = 5$. So the precondition is $\{x = 5\}$.

### More Assignment Examples

<DisplayMath formula="\{x + 1 > 0\} \quad y = x + 1 \quad \{y > 0\}" />

Substitute $x + 1$ for $y$ in $\{y > 0\}$: get $\{x + 1 > 0\}$. This simplifies to $\{x > -1\}$.

<DisplayMath formula="\{(x + 1)^2 = 9\} \quad y = x + 1 \quad \{y^2 = 9\}" />

Substitute $x + 1$ for $y$ in $\{y^2 = 9\}$: get $\{(x+1)^2 = 9\}$, which means $x = 2$ or $x = -4$.

> The proper substitution operator $[x/e]$ is the same operation we formalized in LN 4 for the lambda calculus. The assignment axiom and beta reduction share the same core mechanism: replace a name with a value. The difference is context — in lambda calculus, substitution defines computation; in Hoare Logic, substitution defines proof.

## Building Proofs: The Remaining Rules

### The Sequencing Rule

<DefinitionBox term="Sequencing Rule">

For sequential statements:

<DisplayMath formula="\frac{\{P\} \; C_1 \; \{R\} \quad \{R\} \; C_2 \; \{Q\}}{\{P\} \; C_1;\, C_2 \; \{Q\}}" />

The intermediate assertion $R$ is the "handoff" between the two statements — $C_1$'s postcondition becomes $C_2$'s precondition.

</DefinitionBox>

This rule mirrors LN 12's sequencing equation $S\llbracket s_1; s_2 \rrbracket = S\llbracket s_2 \rrbracket \circ S\llbracket s_1 \rrbracket$. Both express the same idea: sequential execution threads through an intermediate state.

### The Conditional Rule

<DefinitionBox term="Conditional Rule">

<DisplayMath formula="\frac{\{P \wedge B\} \; C_1 \; \{Q\} \quad \{P \wedge \neg B\} \; C_2 \; \{Q\}}{\{P\} \; \textbf{if}\; B \;\textbf{then}\; C_1 \;\textbf{else}\; C_2 \; \{Q\}}" />

Both branches must establish the same postcondition $Q$. The condition $B$ is added to the precondition for the then-branch and negated for the else-branch.

</DefinitionBox>

### The Consequence Rule

<DefinitionBox term="Consequence Rule">

<DisplayMath formula="\frac{P' \implies P \quad \{P\} \; C \; \{Q\} \quad Q \implies Q'}{\{P'\} \; C \; \{Q'\}}" />

If we can strengthen the precondition (from $P$ to $P'$ where $P'$ implies $P$) or weaken the postcondition (from $Q$ to $Q'$ where $Q$ implies $Q'$), the triple still holds. This rule is essential for connecting proofs — it lets us adapt preconditions and postconditions to fit together.

</DefinitionBox>

> The consequence rule is what makes Hoare Logic flexible enough for real proofs. Without it, we'd need the exact precondition and postcondition produced by the other rules. With it, we can strengthen assumptions and weaken guarantees — a principle familiar from LN 2's logical implication.

### The Loop Rule

<DefinitionBox term="Loop Rule">

<DisplayMath formula="\frac{\{I \wedge B\} \; C \; \{I\}}{\{I\} \; \textbf{while}\; B \;\textbf{do}\; C \; \{I \wedge \neg B\}}" />

Here $I$ is the **loop invariant** — a property that holds before and after each iteration. When the loop terminates (because $B$ is false), both $I$ and $\neg B$ hold.

</DefinitionBox>

Notice: these rules look exactly like the inference rules from LN 2 and LN 11. Hoare Logic IS logical deduction applied to programs. The rules are axioms and inference rules of a formal system; a proof of program correctness is a derivation in that system.

## Weakest Preconditions

<DefinitionBox term="Weakest Precondition">

Given a postcondition $Q$ and a command $C$, the **weakest precondition** $\text{wp}(C, Q)$ is the *least restrictive* precondition that guarantees $Q$ after executing $C$. Any other valid precondition implies it: if $\{P\} \; C \; \{Q\}$ is valid, then $P \implies \text{wp}(C, Q)$.

</DefinitionBox>

The assignment axiom directly computes weakest preconditions:

<DisplayMath formula="\text{wp}(x = e, Q) = Q[x / e]" />

For sequential statements:

<DisplayMath formula="\text{wp}(C_1; C_2, Q) = \text{wp}(C_1, \text{wp}(C_2, Q))" />

This works **backward** through the program: start with the desired postcondition, propagate it through each statement from last to first, and arrive at the weakest precondition for the entire program. If that precondition is $\text{true}$, the program is correct for all inputs.

> Edsger Dijkstra formalized weakest preconditions in his 1975 book *A Discipline of Programming*. He saw wp as a way to make program derivation mechanical: instead of writing a program and then verifying it, you start with the desired postcondition and *derive* the program that achieves it. This "program construction by stepwise refinement" influenced an entire generation of software engineers.

### What If We Picked the Wrong Postcondition?

Every example so far ended with the precondition simplifying to `true` — the program achieves the postcondition for *every* input. But wp doesn't only confirm successful proofs. It is a logical reasoner that produces three shapes of result, and we've only seen the first one. Let's run the *same program* against a *wrong* postcondition $\{y = 7\}$ and watch wp catch the lie:

      description: "Apply the assignment axiom: substitute (x + 1) for y in the postcondition. To establish y = 7, the previous statement must leave x + 1 = 7 — i.e., x = 6."
    },
    {
      programLines: [

This counter-example exposes the second of three result shapes wp can produce:

- **Precondition simplifies to `true`** → the program is correct for *all* inputs. (The first proof above.)
- **Precondition simplifies to `false`** → the postcondition is *impossible*; no input makes the program satisfy it. (The counter-example you just walked through.)
- **Precondition simplifies to a non-trivial formula** → the program is correct *only* on the inputs satisfying that formula. (You'll see this shape next, in the malformed-swap example.)

In all three cases wp succeeds at its job; what differs is the verdict. Mechanical backward reasoning gives you a precise answer no matter which shape applies.

> Compare this to the original three-line swap above. Both programs read the same number of lines; both look like swaps. The wp traversal is the same backward walk in both cases — but the *result* is dramatically different. Code review with wp in mind is review with a logical X-ray on.

## Loop Invariants

The loop rule introduces the **invariant** $I$ — a property that must hold before and after every iteration. Finding the right invariant is the key challenge (and the creative part) of axiomatic reasoning.

<DefinitionBox term="Loop Invariant">

A **loop invariant** is a logical predicate $I$ that satisfies:
1. $I$ holds before the loop begins (initialization)
2. If $I$ holds before an iteration and the loop condition $B$ is true, then $I$ holds after the iteration (preservation)
3. When the loop terminates, $I \wedge \neg B$ implies the desired postcondition

</DefinitionBox>

Consider this Petal program that computes the sum $1 + 2 + \ldots + n$:

```
s = 0;
i = 1;
while i <= n do
  s = s + i;
  i = i + 1;
```

We want to prove: $\{n \geq 0\} \; \text{program} \; \{s = n(n+1)/2\}$.

What invariant should we use? The `LoopInvariantChecker` below walks both attempts in sequence — first the obvious-looking guess (which fails), then the correct one. > Finding invariants requires understanding *what the loop is computing at each stage* — it's the property preserved by each iteration, exactly like the property preserved by each approximation in the fixed-point construction from LN 12. ### Finding Invariants: A Systematic Approach

Finding invariants is often called the "creative" part of program verification, but there's a systematic heuristic that works for many loops:

1. **Look at the postcondition.** What property do you want to hold when the loop ends?
2. **Generalize it.** Replace the "final" value with the loop variable. The postcondition says $s = n(n+1)/2$; the generalization is $s = i(i-1)/2$.
3. **Add bounds.** The loop variable has known bounds: $1 \leq i \leq n + 1$.
4. **Verify.** Check that the invariant is (a) true before the loop, (b) preserved by each iteration, and (c) sufficient to prove the postcondition when combined with the negation of the loop condition.

> This heuristic works because loops compute their results incrementally. The invariant captures the "progress so far" — at step $k$, the loop has processed $k$ items and accumulated a partial result. This is the same idea as the denotational approximation sequence (LN 12): each $w_k$ handles $k$ iterations, and the invariant holds for every $w_k$.

## The Blueprint: From Tree Grammar to Verification

Now that we've seen weakest preconditions in action — both on straight-line code and loops — we can step back and see the bigger pattern. In LN 11, we discovered that the tree grammar is an API contract: each production demands one inference rule. In LN 12, each production demanded one semantic equation. The pattern repeats here — each production demands one **weakest precondition definition**.

### Recalling the Tree Grammar

<DisplayMath formula="e : \text{Exp} = n \mid i \mid \text{-}e \mid e\text{+}e \mid e\text{-}e \mid e\text{*}e \mid e\text{/}e \mid e\text{\%}e \mid e\text{**}e" />

<DisplayMath formula="s : \text{Stmt} = i\text{=}e \mid \textbf{print}\; e \mid \textbf{if}\; e\; \textbf{then}\; s^+\; \textbf{else}\; s^+ \mid \textbf{while}\; e\; \textbf{do}\; s^+" />

<DisplayMath formula="p : \text{Program} = s^+" />

Each production is a syntactic category — a type of AST node. For each node type, we defined what it means to propagate a postcondition *backward* through it. This is the wp function.

### Three Lectures, One Pattern

| Tree Grammar Node | LN 11: Inference Rule | LN 12: Semantic Equation | LN 13: wp Definition |
|---|---|---|---|
| $i = e$ | $\langle i = e, \sigma \rangle \to \sigma[i \mapsto v]$ | <InlineMath formula="$S\llbracket i = e \rrbracket\, (\sigma, o) = (\sigma[i \mapsto E\llbracket e \rrbracket\, \sigma], o)$" /> | <InlineMath formula="$\text{wp}(i = e, Q) = Q[i/e]$" /> |
| $s_1;\, s_2$ | Sequencing rules | $S\llbracket s_2 \rrbracket \circ S\llbracket s_1 \rrbracket$ | $\text{wp}(s_1; s_2, Q) = \text{wp}(s_1, \text{wp}(s_2, Q))$ |
| **if** | Conditional rules | Conditional equation | Conditional wp |
| **while** | Loop rules | $\text{fix}(F)$ | Requires invariant $I$ |

The tree grammar is the checklist. When you've written a wp definition for every production, you've completed the axiomatic semantics of the language.

### The Verification Condition Generator

In practice, the wp function is implemented as a **Verification Condition Generator (VCG)** — a recursive function that walks the AST backward, just as the interpreter (LN 11) walks it forward and the transpiler (LN 12) walks it compositionally:

```javascript
function wp(node, Q) {
  switch (node.type) {
    case "Assign":
      return substitute(Q, node.variable, node.expression)
    case "Seq":
      return wp(node.first, wp(node.second, Q))
    case "If":
      const thenWP = wp(node.thenBranch, Q)
      const elseWP = wp(node.elseBranch, Q)
      return and(implies(node.condition, thenWP),
                 implies(not(node.condition), elseWP))
    case "While":
      return node.invariant
  }
}
```

> Compare this function to the `evaluate()` interpreter from LN 11 and the `compile()` transpiler from LN 12. All three are recursive AST traversals. The interpreter produces *values*, the transpiler produces *code*, and the VCG produces *logical formulas*. Same tree, three different outputs — three perspectives on the same language.

<DefinitionBox term="Verification Condition Generator">

A **Verification Condition Generator (VCG)** is a program that traverses the AST backward, applying Hoare rules mechanically to compute the weakest precondition. If the final precondition is implied by the given precondition, the program is correct. The VCG is the axiomatic analog of the interpreter and transpiler.

</DefinitionBox>

### The Complete wp Table

Here is the wp definition for every Petal statement type in one place:

<DisplayMath formula="\text{wp}(i = e,\; Q) = Q[i / e]" />

<DisplayMath formula="\text{wp}(s_1;\, s_2,\; Q) = \text{wp}(s_1,\; \text{wp}(s_2,\; Q))" />

<DisplayMath formula="\text{wp}(\textbf{if}\; e\; \textbf{then}\; C_1\; \textbf{else}\; C_2,\; Q) = (e \Rightarrow \text{wp}(C_1, Q)) \;\wedge\; (\neg e \Rightarrow \text{wp}(C_2, Q))" />

Print does not modify the environment, so the precondition is the postcondition itself. The output stream changes, but Hoare Logic (in its standard form) tracks logical assertions about program *variables*, not I/O.

## Partial vs. Total Correctness

There's a subtlety we've been glossing over. The Hoare triple $\{P\} \; C \; \{Q\}$ says: *if* $P$ holds and $C$ **terminates**, *then* $Q$ holds. But what if $C$ doesn't terminate?

Consider:
<DisplayMath formula="\{\text{true}\} \quad \textbf{while}\; 1\; \textbf{do}\; \textbf{skip} \quad \{x = 42\}" />

This triple is **valid** under partial correctness! The loop never terminates, so the postcondition is vacuously satisfied — it's an "if-then" with a false antecedent.

<DefinitionBox term="Partial vs. Total Correctness">

- **Partial correctness** ($\{P\} \; C \; \{Q\}$): IF $C$ terminates, THEN $Q$ holds.
- **Total correctness** ($[P] \; C \; [Q]$): $C$ TERMINATES and $Q$ holds.

Proving total correctness requires an additional ingredient: a **variant** (also called a "ranking function") — a quantity that decreases on each iteration and is bounded below, guaranteeing termination.

</DefinitionBox>

<DefinitionBox term="Variant (Ranking Function)">

A **variant** is an expression $V$ that:
1. Is a non-negative integer (or ordinal) — bounded below by 0
2. Strictly decreases with each loop iteration

If such a $V$ exists, the loop must terminate: it can decrease at most $V_0$ times before reaching 0.

</DefinitionBox>

For our summation loop, the variant is $V = n + 1 - i$. It starts at $n$ and decreases by 1 each iteration, reaching 0 when $i = n + 1$ (at which point $i > n$, so the loop exits).

For the exponentiation loop, the variant is $V = exp - count$.

## Loops at the Limit of Every Framework

Step back across the last three lectures and notice something striking: **loops were the rough patch in every single framework we built.** It is not that each framework happened to be awkward in a different place; it is the same wall, hit from three different directions.

| Framework | Loop in the easy case | Loop in the general case |
|-----------|-----------------------|---------------------------|
| Operational (LN 11) | Repeated unfolding via `while-true` and `while-false` rules | Non-termination = "no derivation tree exists" — you cannot read termination off the rules; you have to actually try and wait |
| Denotational (LN 12) | Straight-line code: one semantic equation per construct | Loops require a *fixed point* (Kleene's theorem). The meaning is the LIMIT of an infinite approximation tower, which in general you cannot compute in finite time |
| Axiomatic (LN 13) | Assignment, sequencing, conditional all run mechanically | Loops require a *human-supplied* invariant. The loop rule is the only Hoare rule the VCG cannot apply on its own |

Each framework reveals a different facet of the same difficulty, but the underlying cause is one thing.

### The Common Cause: Rice's Theorem

<DefinitionBox term="Rice's Theorem (LN 5)">

For any **non-trivial semantic property** of programs in a Turing-complete language, the question "does program $P$ have property $X$?" is **undecidable**. There is no algorithm that always halts and gives the correct yes/no answer for every program $P$.

</DefinitionBox>

"Non-trivial" means the property is true for some programs and false for others (i.e., not the always-true or always-false properties). "Semantic" means the property depends on what the program *does*, not on syntactic features like its length. Concrete examples of properties Rice's Theorem rules out:

- **Termination:** "Does $P$ halt on all inputs?" — undecidable.
- **Equivalence:** "Do $P_1$ and $P_2$ compute the same function?" — undecidable.
- **Output range:** "Does $P$ ever return a negative number?" — undecidable.
- **Dead code:** "Does line 17 of $P$ ever execute?" — undecidable.
- **Loop bounds:** "Does this loop always finish in fewer than $N$ steps?" — undecidable in general.

The Halting Problem is the canonical instance, but Rice's Theorem says the trouble is much wider than just halting: *almost any* interesting question about Turing-complete programs is undecidable.

Loops are the bridge to Turing-completeness. A language with only assignment, sequencing, and conditionals is **not** Turing-complete and avoids Rice's Theorem entirely — but it cannot express algorithms that need an unknown number of iterations. The moment you add unbounded `while`, you cross the line, and from that moment on every framework that wants to *automate* its analysis hits a wall on loops specifically.

### The Same Escape Hatch Three Times

Each framework has the same way out, just expressed in its own dialect: **restrict the syntax so the loop's iteration count is statically bounded.** Then every framework's loop problem becomes solvable:

- **Operational:** evaluation is guaranteed to terminate, so "no derivation" cannot happen for syntactic reasons.
- **Denotational:** the approximation tower's least upper bound is reached in finitely many steps, so the meaning is computable.
- **Axiomatic:** the VCG can derive the invariant and variant automatically from the loop bound.

The `bounded_loop` extension you are about to see is exactly this trick. It is not a convenience feature; it is the only known general escape from the loop wall, repackaged for the axiomatic setting. The same trick recurs every time a real language wants decidable properties: SQL refuses unbounded loops to keep query optimisation tractable, GLSL bounds shader loops to avoid GPU hangs, Coq enforces structural recursion to keep proof checking decidable. Different languages, same trade.

## Extending Petal: Total Correctness by Design

In LN 11 and LN 12, we saw that adding a feature to Petal activates the entire pipeline: concrete syntax, grammar, tree grammar, semantic rules, and (if needed) new foundational mathematics. The same pipeline applies here — adding a feature requires a new wp definition and, potentially, a new proof obligation.

But axiomatic semantics reveals a deeper insight: **language design choices can give you free proofs.**

### The Problem with `while`

Petal's `while` loop is Turing-complete — it can express any computable function, including non-terminating ones. This means we *cannot* automatically prove total correctness for programs with `while`. Every loop requires a human-supplied invariant and variant. The VCG can verify the proof, but it cannot *find* it.

### The `bounded_loop` Solution

Suppose we add a new construct to Petal's tree grammar:

<DisplayMath formula="s : \text{Stmt} = \ldots \mid \textbf{for}\; i\; \textbf{in}\; \textbf{range}(e)\; \textbf{do}\; s^+" />

This `for` loop iterates exactly $E\llbracket e \rrbracket$ times. The loop variable $i$ counts from 0 to $e - 1$, and the body cannot modify $i$. The wp definition is:

<DisplayMath formula="\text{wp}(\textbf{for}\; i\; \textbf{in}\; \textbf{range}(e)\; \textbf{do}\; C,\; Q) = \forall\, 0 \leq i < e.\; \text{wp}(C, Q)" />

The variant is automatically $e - i$ — it decreases by 1 each iteration and is bounded below by 0. No human needs to supply it. **Total correctness is guaranteed by the syntax.**

### Trading Expressiveness for Safety

This is not a hypothetical idea. Many real languages make exactly this trade-off:

| Language | Restriction | Guarantee |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| SQL | No unbounded loops | All queries terminate |
| Datalog | Stratified negation, no function symbols | All programs terminate |
| GLSL (shaders) | Bounded loop counts only | No GPU hangs |
| Coq / Agda | Structural recursion only | All functions terminate |
| Idris | Totality checker | Warns on potentially non-terminating code |

> The Halting Problem (LN 5) says we cannot decide termination for Turing-complete languages. But if we restrict the grammar — removing unbounded loops, requiring structural recursion, or enforcing bounded iteration — we move from Turing-complete (Type 0) toward more restricted Chomsky hierarchy levels (LN 7), gaining decidable properties in exchange. Axiomatic semantics makes this trade-off explicit: the simpler the syntax, the more powerful the proofs.

## Real-World Static Verification

Axiomatic semantics might seem like a mathematical exercise, but its applications are everywhere — often in disguise.

### Type Checkers as Lightweight Axiomatics

Every time TypeScript rejects `const x: number = "hello"`, it's applying an axiomatic rule. The type annotation `number` is a precondition: "this variable holds a number." The type checker verifies that all uses of `x` are consistent with this annotation — without running the program.

```typescript
// TypeScript: the type annotation IS a Hoare-style assertion
function double(x: number): number {  // {x: number} → {result: number}
  return x * 2;
}

// The type checker proves: {x: number} x * 2 {result: number}
// This is a restricted Hoare triple verified at compile time
```

Type systems are **restricted Hoare Logics** where the "assertions" are types and the "proof" is type checking. The restriction (only tracking types, not arbitrary properties) makes the system **decidable** — type checking always terminates. This is a pragmatic trade-off (LN 6): we sacrifice expressiveness (can't prove `y = 6`) for decidability (the checker always gives an answer).

### Rust's Borrow Checker

Rust's borrow checker is a more sophisticated static verification system. It proves at compile time that:
- No data races (at most one mutable reference at a time)
- No use-after-free (references don't outlive their data)
- No null pointer dereferences (the `Option` type forces explicit handling)

```rust
fn main() {
    let mut data = vec![1, 2, 3];
    let r = &data;          // immutable borrow
    // data.push(4);        // ERROR: can't mutate while borrowed
    println!("{:?}", r);    // borrow ends here
    data.push(4);           // OK: borrow has ended
}
```

These are Hoare-style properties: "after this line, no other reference can mutate this data" is a postcondition that the compiler verifies for every borrow. The result: **memory safety without a garbage collector**, verified before a single line executes.

> Rust's borrow checker proves that **ownership** — the right to read, write, or deallocate data — is always well-defined. This is an axiomatic guarantee about the program's memory behavior. The compiler rejects programs that might violate ownership rules, just as Hoare Logic rejects programs whose preconditions don't imply the desired postconditions.

### Industrial Formal Verification

When the stakes are highest, full axiomatic verification is employed:

- **SPARK Ada** (aviation): used in the DO-178C standard for flight control software. Hoare-style contracts are written alongside the code, and the SPARK prover verifies them automatically.
- **Frama-C** (nuclear): used to verify C code in safety-critical nuclear systems. Annotations are written in ACSL (ANSI/ISO C Specification Language), a Hoare-style assertion language.
- **CompCert** (compilers): a C compiler written and verified in the Coq proof assistant. The compiler itself is *proven correct* — the compiled output is guaranteed to have the same semantics as the source. This is the ultimate synthesis of LN 9 (parsing), LN 11 (operational semantics), and today's verification.
- **seL4** (operating systems): a formally verified microkernel used in critical defense and infrastructure systems. Every line of code is proven correct against a formal specification.

Dafny makes Hoare triples native syntax. Students seeing this for the first time often realize they've been writing informal contracts all along:

```csharp
// Dafny: Hoare triples as language keywords
method Abs(x: int) returns (y: int)
  requires true          // precondition: accepts any integer
  ensures y >= 0         // postcondition: result is non-negative
  ensures y == x || y == -x
{
  if x < 0 { y := -x; } else { y := x; }
}
```

The `requires` keyword IS the precondition $P$; `ensures` IS the postcondition $Q$. The Dafny verifier is a VCG — it walks the method body backward, computing weakest preconditions, and checks that the `requires` clause implies the computed wp. The compiler rejects the program if the proof fails.

> These tools don't eliminate all bugs, but they provide **mathematical guarantees** about specific properties. The Halting Problem (LN 5) means we can't prove everything, but within the decidable fragment, we can prove remarkable things. When a verified compiler produces code, you know the code is correct — not because you tested it, but because you proved it.

### Design by Contract

<DefinitionBox term="Design by Contract">

**Design by Contract** (DbC) is a software design methodology where functions specify their preconditions, postconditions, and invariants as part of their interface. The "contract" is a Hoare triple: the caller guarantees the precondition, the function guarantees the postcondition.

</DefinitionBox>

```python
def binary_search(arr, target):
    """
    Contract:
    Pre:  arr is sorted in ascending order
    Post: returns index i where arr[i] == target, or -1 if not found
    Invariant: target is in arr[lo..hi] if it exists
    """
    lo, hi = 0, len(arr) - 1
    while lo <= hi:
        mid = (lo + hi) // 2
        if arr[mid] == target:
            return mid
        elif arr[mid] < target:
            lo = mid + 1
        else:
            hi = mid - 1
    return -1
```

The docstring is an informal Hoare triple. Languages like Eiffel and D build contracts into the language syntax; others use assertion libraries or documentation conventions.

### Static Analysis Tools

Even without full formal verification, many tools use axiomatic reasoning to catch bugs:

```javascript
// ESLint: enforces code conventions (a very weak form of axiomatics)
// "if x is undefined, accessing x.property is an error"
const y = x.property;  // ESLint warns if x might be null

// Flow / TypeScript: type-level assertions
function add(a: number, b: number): number {
  return a + b;  // compiler proves return type matches
}
```

```rust
// Clippy: Rust's linter uses ownership axioms
let v = vec![1, 2, 3];
let first = &v[0];
v.push(4);  // Clippy + compiler: ERROR — v is borrowed
println!("{}", first);
```

```python
# mypy: gradual typing for Python
def greet(name: str) -> str:
    return "Hello, " + name

greet(42)  # mypy error: expected str, got int
```

These tools exist on a spectrum from very simple (linters checking naming conventions) to very powerful (Rust's borrow checker proving memory safety). All of them are forms of axiomatic reasoning: establishing preconditions and postconditions and verifying that the code satisfies them.

## The Broader Landscape

Operational, denotational, and axiomatic semantics are the "big three," but the field has developed several more frameworks, each with unique strengths.

### Algebraic Semantics

<DefinitionBox term="Algebraic Semantics">

**Algebraic semantics** defines meaning through **equations between terms**. An abstract data type (ADT) is specified by a signature (the operations available) and axioms (equational properties). The meaning is the **initial algebra** — the most constrained interpretation satisfying all axioms.

</DefinitionBox>

For example, a stack is specified by:

| Operation | Axiom |
|-----------|-------|
| `top(push(s, x))` | `= x` |
| `pop(push(s, x))` | `= s` |
| `isEmpty(empty)` | `= true` |
| `isEmpty(push(s, x))` | `= false` |

These equations define the meaning of stack operations without describing any implementation. Any data structure that satisfies these axioms IS a stack — whether it's an array, a linked list, or a cons cell. This connects to LN 3: algebras generalize set-theoretic structures by adding operations and equations.

### Algebras You've Already Been Writing

You may not have realised it, but you have been writing partial algebraic specifications for years — every time you declared an interface, trait, type class, or protocol. Compare the same `Stack` algebra rendered in three popular languages:

```typescript
// TypeScript — interface
interface Stack<T> {
  push(value: T): void;
  pop(): T | undefined;
  top(): T | undefined;
  isEmpty(): boolean;
}
```

```rust
// Rust — trait
trait Stack<T> {
    fn push(&mut self, value: T);
    fn pop(&mut self) -> Option<T>;
    fn top(&self) -> Option<&T>;
    fn is_empty(&self) -> bool;
}
```

```haskell
-- Haskell — type class
class Stack s where
  push    :: a -> s a -> s a
  pop     :: s a -> Maybe (s a)
  top     :: s a -> Maybe a
  isEmpty :: s a -> Bool
```

Each declaration is the **signature** half of an algebra: the operations available. The four equations from the table above are the **axioms**: the semantic content. Most production type systems let you specify the signature but not the axioms, so the axioms get encoded out-of-band — in documentation, in unit tests, or in property-based testing libraries (QuickCheck, Hypothesis, fast-check) that *generate* values exercising the axioms. When the axioms ARE encoded in the type system itself — Liquid Haskell's refinement types, Idris's dependent types, Lean's tactic proofs — you have full algebraic semantics available at compile time.

Either way, every interface you have ever shipped was an algebraic specification with the equations sitting outside the type system. The instinct that "an interface is a contract" is not a metaphor; it is a literal statement about which operations and which equations the implementor must satisfy.

### Action Semantics

Peter Mosses developed Action Semantics in 1992 to address a practical problem: operational and denotational semantics don't scale well to full language specifications. The Standard ML specification is hundreds of pages of denotational semantics — readable only by experts.

Action Semantics uses three "facets" — functional, imperative, and declarative — to decompose a language's semantics into independently understandable parts. Its English-like combinators make it remarkably readable compared to denotational notation:

```
-- Action Semantics notation (Mosses, 1992)
-- Meaning of "x = e":
  evaluate e
  then
  bind x to the given value
```

Compare this to the denotational equation <InlineMath formula="$S\llbracket i = e \rrbracket\, (\sigma, o) = (\sigma[i \mapsto E\llbracket e \rrbracket\, \sigma], o)$" />. Both say the same thing, but Action Semantics reads like a recipe while denotational semantics reads like an equation. The trade-off: Action Semantics is more readable but less amenable to mathematical proof.

### Modern Directions

The frontier of semantics keeps growing. Each of the four directions below addresses something the "big three" handle awkwardly or not at all — and each has at least one concrete language or library carrying it into practice today.

#### Game Semantics

The intuition: a program's meaning is a **strategy** in a back-and-forth game between two players. **Player P** is the program; **Opponent O** is the environment that calls into the program. A function call is opened by O ("here is an input"), and P must reply ("here is the output"). Composition of programs is composition of strategies; type-correctness is the rule that strategies stay legal moves in the game's protocol. Game semantics excels at describing **interactive** computation — input/output, networking, GUIs, message passing — that denotational semantics struggles to model with plain functions.

The smallest example: the function `f(x) = x + 1`, viewed as a play of the game.

The whole interaction is a *strategy*: a recipe telling P how to respond to whatever O does. Two programs have the same meaning in game semantics iff they describe the same strategy. This sounds abstract, but it has been the foundation of important results in concurrency theory and full abstraction proofs for languages like PCF and Idealised Algol — places where denotational semantics couldn't quite line up with operational behaviour, and game semantics did.

> A natural question to ask is whether game semantics has any connection to the **minimax** algorithm you may have seen in AI or game-playing courses. The honest answer is: only a loose family resemblance. Both involve two alternating "players," and both compute outcomes recursively over a tree of moves, but they answer different questions. Minimax assumes the two players are adversarial and computes the optimal play under that assumption. Game semantics treats P and O as *protocol participants* whose moves are constrained by types and by the program's strategy, not by any maximisation. The kinship is structural, not algorithmic — which is worth knowing if you ever try to read a game-semantics paper after a course on adversarial search.

#### Categorical Semantics

Meaning is given in terms of **category theory** — objects, morphisms, functors, natural transformations, monads. A program's type is an object; a program is a morphism. Side effects are captured by monads (`Maybe`, `State`, `IO`); composition is morphism composition. The pay-off is structural: equivalences between languages become functors between categories, and many otherwise-mysterious correspondences (continuations and double negation, dependent types and locally cartesian closed categories) drop out as theorems.

The cost is steep mathematical background. Most students will encounter categorical semantics indirectly: the `IO` monad in Haskell, the `Promise` monad in JavaScript, the `do`-notation in F# — each of these IS a categorical construction made usable by language sugar. You have already programmed in categorical semantics; you just have not yet seen the diagrams.

#### Probabilistic Semantics

A program with `x := flip(0.5)` does not have a single meaning — it has a **distribution** over meanings. Probabilistic semantics formalises this: the denotation of a program is a probability measure over output states (or, in continuous settings, a measure-theoretic kernel). It is the mathematical foundation for everything from machine learning training loops (gradient descent on log-likelihoods) to MCMC samplers, Bayesian inference engines, and differential privacy guarantees. Languages that take this seriously — Stan, Pyro, Edward, Gen, Anglican — let you write a generative model as a program and ask the language to *invert* it via inference. The semantics has to know what "invert" means, which is exactly what probabilistic semantics provides.

#### Algebraic Effects

A modern approach to tracking side effects in the **type system itself**. Gaining traction as a replacement for monads in OCaml 5, Koka, Eff, and recent research lines for Haskell. The idea: declare effect operations as a kind of "interface for side effects," and let call sites *handle* them with arbitrary interpretations.

```
// Koka: algebraic effects (2024)
effect ask {
  fun prompt(msg: string): string
}

fun greet(): ask string {
  val name = prompt("What is your name?")
  "Hello, " ++ name
}

fun main() {
  with handler {
    fun prompt(msg) { "World" }
  }
  println(greet())  // prints "Hello, World"
}
```

In Koka, effects are declared in the type signature and *handled* at the call site. The `ask` effect is a type-level assertion — it declares what side effects the function *may* perform. The handler provides the implementation, separating the *what* (the effect) from the *how* (the handler). This is an axiomatic-style declaration: "this function requires an `ask` capability" is a precondition verified at compile time.

### The Three Approaches: A Unified View

Now that we've seen all three approaches, we can appreciate how they're related. They're not competing theories — they're different perspectives on the same mathematical reality:

<DisplayMath formula="\text{Operational} \xrightarrow{\text{adequacy}} \text{Denotational} \xrightarrow{\text{soundness}} \text{Axiomatic}" />

- **Adequacy** (LN 12): the operational and denotational semantics agree — they produce the same results for terminating programs.
- **Soundness**: if a Hoare triple is derivable using the axiomatic rules, then it is true according to the operational/denotational semantics. The axiomatic system only proves correct things.

The reverse directions are also important:
- **Completeness**: if a Hoare triple is true (according to operational/denotational semantics), can it always be proved using the axiomatic rules? Cook showed in 1978 that Hoare Logic is **relatively complete** — it can prove any true triple, as long as the underlying assertion logic is expressive enough. But the assertion logic itself may be undecidable, which connects back to Rice's Theorem (LN 5).

> This triangle of relationships shows that the three approaches are consistent with each other. They define the same "meaning" — just described in different ways. This consistency is not accidental; it's a deep mathematical fact about the nature of computation.

These are not three competing descriptions; they are three views of one mathematical object. **Real toolchains use all three at once.** A debugger thinks operationally — show me the next step. ## Choosing the Right Tool

No single semantic approach is "best." Each illuminates different aspects of meaning:

| Goal | Best Approach |
|------|---------------|
| Debugging, understanding execution | Operational |
| Compiler optimization, equivalence proofs | Denotational |
| Program verification, safety guarantees | Axiomatic |
| Library/API specification | Algebraic |
| Full language specification | Action or Operational |
| Theoretical foundations | Denotational or Categorical |

The three lectures (LN 11-13) gave you three lenses on the same programs. The interpreter shows what *happens*. The transpiler shows what *function* is computed. The verifier shows what *must be true*. Together, they form a complete toolkit for understanding and implementing programming languages.

Languages do not pick exclusively — every Turing-complete language admits all three semantics, in principle — but their idioms, type systems, error messages, and debugger features lean heavily toward one. ## Summary

Today we completed the trio of semantic approaches with **axiomatic semantics — meaning by proving**. Hoare Logic lets us annotate programs with preconditions and postconditions, then use inference rules to prove properties without execution.

We saw the **blueprint** pattern continue: the tree grammar is the same API contract it was for operational and denotational semantics — each production demands one wp definition. The Verification Condition Generator is just another AST traversal, parallel to the interpreter and the transpiler, producing *logical formulas* instead of values or code. The `WPStepper` made this visible on the simple two-statement example, where the AST view shines.

We learned the assignment axiom (the foundation, running backward), the sequencing rule (chaining proofs), the conditional rule (branching with brace-free `then`/`else`), the consequence rule (adapting proofs), and the loop rule (requiring an invariant with `do`). Crucially, we saw wp produce **all three of its result shapes**: simplifying to `true` (the program is correct for all inputs), simplifying to `false` (the postcondition is impossible — a counter-example refutation, demonstrated on `x = 5; y = x + 1; {y = 7}`), and simplifying to a non-trivial formula (the program is correct only on a specific subset of inputs — demonstrated by the malformed temp-less swap, which only works when its two inputs were already equal).

The redesigned `LoopInvariantChecker` walks both attempts at the summation invariant in sequence — first the obvious-but-too-weak guess `s ≥ 0`, then the correct algebraic invariant — exposing every micro-step of the Initialization, Preservation, and Termination derivations so you can see exactly why one falls apart and the other lands. The same machinery walks the exponentiation invariant under a single attempt.

We pulled together a thread that ran across all three lectures: **loops are the rough patch in every semantic framework**. Operational semantics stumbles on non-termination, denotational semantics needs an infinite fixed-point construction, and axiomatic semantics requires a human-supplied invariant. The common cause is Rice's Theorem: any non-trivial semantic property of Turing-complete programs is undecidable. The same escape hatch — bounding the loop's iteration count statically — fixes the problem in all three frameworks at once. The `bounded_loop` extension, SQL's refusal of unbounded loops, GLSL's bounded shader loops, and Coq's structural recursion are all instances of the same trade.

We saw axiomatic reasoning at work in industry: TypeScript's type checker, Rust's borrow checker, Dafny's built-in Hoare triples, SPARK Ada's prover, CompCert's verified compiler, and the seL4 verified microkernel. We surveyed the broader landscape — algebraic semantics as the math behind every interface, trait, and type class you have ever written; action semantics with its readable English-like notation; game semantics as interactive strategy (with a brief note distinguishing it from minimax); categorical semantics as the structural backbone of monadic effects; probabilistic semantics for randomised computation; and algebraic effects in Koka.

Finally, the `ThreeLensesStepper` showed the same Petal program through operational, denotational, and axiomatic eyes in turn, then all three at once — making the "they agree" claim concrete instead of abstract. The `LanguageExplorer` then turned the lens on languages themselves, plotting Python, Haskell, Rust, Coq/Lean/Agda, SQL, C, and Prolog along the operational/denotational/axiomatic axes to show that real languages have a **semantic centre of gravity** which shapes how their idioms read.

Next lecture, we bring everything together: the **full compilation pipeline**, mapping every stage to the lecture that taught it. Petal completes its journey from design to execution to verification.
