## The Seven Bridges of Königsberg

> **Historical Note:** In 1736, a 29-year-old mathematician named **Leonhard Euler** was living in Saint Petersburg, Russia. Born in Basel, Switzerland, he had studied under the legendary Johann Bernoulli and moved east at age 20 to join the Imperial Russian Academy of Sciences. He was already prolific — publishing papers on number theory, mechanics, and optics. By the end of his life he would publish over 800 papers, more than any mathematician in history, continuing even after going blind in both eyes.

But a puzzle from a nearby city caught his attention.

**Königsberg** (modern-day Kaliningrad, Russia) sits on the Pregel River. The city center includes two large islands connected to each other and to the two riverbanks by **seven bridges**. The residents had a long-standing question: could you walk through the city crossing every bridge exactly once, without backtracking over any bridge you've already used?

Here is a map of the city's layout:

<img src="/cmsi-2820/notes/LN20-Konigsberg.jpg" alt="Historical map of Königsberg showing the four landmasses and seven bridges across the Pregel River" className="my-6 rounded-lg border" />

> **Key Insight:** Try to trace a path that crosses every edge exactly once. No matter how you approach it, you'll find it impossible. But *why*? What makes this particular arrangement fundamentally different from one where it *would* work?

To answer this, Euler didn't just solve the puzzle — he invented an entirely new branch of mathematics. He stripped away the geography, the river, the specific layout of the city, and focused on the only thing that mattered: *which landmasses are connected to which, and how many connections each one has*. He called this *geometria situs* — the geometry of position. Today we call it **graph theory**.

> **Historical Note:** The word "graph" wasn't coined until 1878, when **James Joseph Sylvester** used it in a paper about chemical molecules. The vocabulary we use today took over a century to stabilize after Euler's original insight.

## Defining Graphs

Let's formalize what Euler did. He identified two kinds of things: **landmasses** (the objects) and **bridges** (the connections between them). In graph theory, we call these **vertices** and **edges**.

<DefinitionBox term="Graph (Informal)">

A **graph** consists of a set of **vertices** (also called nodes) and a set of **edges** connecting pairs of vertices.

An **undirected edge** connects two vertices without any direction — you can traverse it either way (like a bridge). A **directed edge** (or **arc**) goes from one vertex to another — it has a start and an end (like a one-way street).

</DefinitionBox>

## Degrees, Walks, and Paths

Now that we have the language of vertices and edges, we need vocabulary for talking about the *structure* around a vertex and the *routes* through a graph.

<DefinitionBox term="Degree">

The **degree** of a vertex $v$, written <InlineMath formula="\deg(v)" />, is the number of edges touching $v$.

In a directed graph, we distinguish:
- **In-degree** <InlineMath formula="\deg^-(v)" />: the number of edges pointing *into* $v$
- **Out-degree** <InlineMath formula="\deg^+(v)" />: the number of edges pointing *out of* $v$

</DefinitionBox>

Next, we need to formalize what it means to "travel through" a graph. There are three levels of rigor:

<DefinitionBox term="Walk, Trail, and Path">

A **walk** is any sequence of vertices connected by edges: <InlineMath formula="v_0, e_1, v_1, e_2, v_2, \ldots, e_k, v_k" />. Edges and vertices may repeat.

A **trail** is a walk with **no repeated edges**. Vertices may still repeat.

A **path** is a walk with **no repeated vertices** (and therefore no repeated edges either).

</DefinitionBox>

Here is a summary of these three traversal types:

| Type | Repeated Edges? | Repeated Vertices? | Strictness |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :--- |
| Walk | Yes | Yes | No restrictions |
| Trail | No | Yes | Edges are unique |
| Path | No | No | Most restrictive |

## Solving Königsberg with the New Vocabulary

Now let's return to the Königsberg puzzle. With our new vocabulary, the question becomes: does the Königsberg multigraph have an **Euler trail** — a trail that uses every edge exactly once?

Euler's key insight was to think about what happens at each vertex during such a trail.

Consider a vertex in the *middle* of the trail (not the start or end). Every time you **arrive** at this vertex via one edge, you must **leave** via a different edge. The edges at this vertex get consumed in arrive/leave pairs. This means **middle vertices must have even degree** — otherwise you'd arrive one final time with no unused edge to leave by.

What about the **start** and **end** vertices? The start vertex has one extra "leave" (the first move), and the end vertex has one extra "arrive" (the last move). So these may have **odd degree**.

This gives us **Euler's theorem** for trails:

<DefinitionBox term="Euler Trail / Euler Circuit">

An **Euler trail** is a trail that uses every edge of the graph exactly once.

An **Euler circuit** is an Euler trail that starts and ends at the same vertex.

- A connected graph has an **Euler circuit** if and only if **every vertex has even degree**
- A connected graph has an **Euler trail** (but not a circuit) if and only if **exactly two vertices have odd degree** — these must be the start and end

</DefinitionBox>

### Solving the Königsberg Problem

Now let's apply Euler's theorem to Königsberg. The degrees of the four landmasses are:

- **Island (A)**: degree 5 (connected by 5 bridges) — **odd**
- **South (B)**: degree 3 — **odd**
- **North (C)**: degree 3 — **odd**
- **East (D)**: degree 3 — **odd**

**All four vertices have odd degree.** For an Euler trail we need exactly 0 or 2 odd-degree vertices. With 4, it is **impossible** to cross every bridge exactly once. Euler proved, with pure logic and no trial-and-error, that the residents' challenge could never be solved.

### Modern Kaliningrad

But how much has the city changed since 1736? Königsberg was heavily bombed in World War II, and today — as Kaliningrad, Russia — some bridges no longer exist.

<img src="/cmsi-2820/notes/LN20-Kaliningrad.png" alt="Modern map of Kaliningrad showing the remaining bridges across the Pregolya River" className="my-6 rounded-lg border" />

With 8 bridges remaining, the degrees are: Island = 3 (odd), South = 4 (even), North = 3 (odd), East = 6 (even). Exactly **two** vertices have odd degree (Island and North). By Euler's theorem, an Euler trail exists — starting at Island and ending at North (or vice versa). The puzzle that was impossible in 1736 is now solvable!

## Formalizing Graphs as Tuples of Sets

So far we've been thinking about graphs visually and informally. But this course is built on **set theory** — and graphs turn out to have a beautifully clean set-theoretic definition that connects directly to the formalism we developed in LN4–5 and LN10.

<DefinitionBox term="Graph (Formal)">

An **undirected graph** is an ordered pair <InlineMath formula="G = (V, E)" /> where:
- <InlineMath formula="V" /> is a finite set of **vertices**
- <InlineMath formula="E \subseteq \{\{u, v\} \mid u, v \in V,\; u \neq v\}" /> is a set of **edges**, each being a 2-element subset of <InlineMath formula="V" />

A **directed graph** (digraph) is an ordered pair <InlineMath formula="G = (V, E)" /> where:
- <InlineMath formula="V" /> is a finite set of vertices
- <InlineMath formula="E \subseteq \{(u, v) \mid u, v \in V\}" /> is a set of **arcs**, each being an ordered pair from <InlineMath formula="V" />

The **order** of a graph is <InlineMath formula="|V|" /> (the number of vertices). The **size** is <InlineMath formula="|E|" /> (the number of edges).

</DefinitionBox>

Notice the difference: undirected edges are **sets** <InlineMath formula="\{u, v\}" /> (order doesn't matter — callbacks to LN9), while directed edges are **tuples** <InlineMath formula="(u, v)" /> (order matters — callbacks to LN8).

## Graph Representations — From Math to Code

We have the mathematical definition. How does a *computer* store a graph? There are three standard representations, and they connect directly to Python data structures you already know.

### Adjacency Matrix

An **adjacency matrix** is an <InlineMath formula="n \times n" /> matrix <InlineMath formula="A" /> where <InlineMath formula="A[i][j] = 1" /> if there is an edge between vertices <InlineMath formula="v_i" /> and <InlineMath formula="v_j" />, and 0 otherwise. For undirected graphs, the matrix is **symmetric** (<InlineMath formula="A[i][j] = A[j][i]" />).

For our 4-vertex graph above:

|       | A | B | C | D |
| :---  |:-:|:-:|:-:|:-:|
| **A** | 0 | 1 | 0 | 1 |
| **B** | 1 | 0 | 1 | 0 |
| **C** | 0 | 1 | 0 | 1 |
| **D** | 1 | 0 | 1 | 0 |

### Adjacency List

An **adjacency list** stores, for each vertex, the set of its neighbors. More space-efficient for **sparse** graphs (few edges relative to vertices):

- A → \{B, D\}
- B → \{A, C\}
- C → \{B, D\}
- D → \{C, A\}

The **trade-offs**: an adjacency matrix gives <InlineMath formula="O(1)" /> edge lookup ("is there an edge between A and C?") but uses <InlineMath formula="O(n^2)" /> space. An adjacency list uses <InlineMath formula="O(n + m)" /> space (where <InlineMath formula="m = |E|" />) but checking for a specific edge takes <InlineMath formula="O(\deg(v))" /> time.

### Graphs in Python

These representations map directly onto Python data structures from LN8 and LN9. Here are all three for the same graph:

```python
V = {"A", "B", "C", "D"}

# Edge set — frozensets for undirected (LN9: hashable, unordered)
E = {frozenset({"A","B"}), frozenset({"B","C"}),
     frozenset({"C","D"}), frozenset({"D","A"})}

# Adjacency list — dict of sets (LN9: sets for neighbors)
adj_list = {
    "A": {"B", "D"},
    "B": {"A", "C"},
    "C": {"B", "D"},
    "D": {"C", "A"},
}

# Adjacency matrix — 2D list
vertices = ["A", "B", "C", "D"]
adj_matrix = [
    [0, 1, 0, 1],  # A
    [1, 0, 1, 0],  # B
    [0, 1, 0, 1],  # C
    [1, 0, 1, 0],  # D
]
```

Notice: the formal definition <InlineMath formula="G = (V, E)" /> with set-pair edges maps directly to `frozenset` (unordered, hashable — LN9). For directed graphs, edges would be `tuple`s instead (ordered — LN8). The adjacency list is a `dict[str, set[str]]` — a dict mapping each vertex to its set of neighbors. Python's `dict` is itself a hash table (callback to LN19), and now we're using it to represent an entirely new mathematical structure.

Computing degree is just `len()`:

```python
def degree(graph, v):
    return len(graph[v])

degree(adj_list, "A")  # 2 — A is connected to B and D
```

This notion of "same structure, different labels" is called **isomorphism**.

<DefinitionBox term="Graph Isomorphism">

Two graphs <InlineMath formula="G_1 = (V_1, E_1)" /> and <InlineMath formula="G_2 = (V_2, E_2)" /> are **isomorphic** (written <InlineMath formula="G_1 \cong G_2" />) if there exists a bijection <InlineMath formula="f: V_1 \to V_2" /> such that:

<DisplayMath formula="\{u, v\} \in E_1 \iff \{f(u), f(v)\} \in E_2" />

The function <InlineMath formula="f" /> is called an **isomorphism**. It relabels vertices while preserving all adjacency relationships.

</DefinitionBox>

This connects directly to LN15 (bijections) and LN19 (bijective proofs): an isomorphism is a *bijection* that preserves graph structure. And like the equivalence relations from LN11, isomorphism is an **equivalence relation** on graphs — it's reflexive, symmetric, and transitive.

Isomorphism is a **weaker form of equality**. Two graphs can be equal (same V, same E — literally identical) or isomorphic (same structure, possibly different labels). In Python terms, think of it as the difference between `==` (LN11's `__eq__` — label-equality) and structural equivalence. Two `dict` graph representations can be `==` without being isomorphic (same labels but we check structure), or isomorphic without being `==` (different labels, same structure).

Now consider these two graphs — they have the same number of vertices (4) and the same number of edges (3), but are they isomorphic?

**No.** The first graph is a path (degree sequence: 1, 2, 2, 1). The second is a star (degree sequence: 3, 1, 1, 1). An isomorphism must preserve degrees — it's a bijection on vertices, so the degree of <InlineMath formula="v" /> equals the degree of <InlineMath formula="f(v)" />. Since the degree sequences differ, no isomorphism can exist.

This gives us a quick necessary condition for isomorphism: **isomorphic graphs must have the same degree sequence**. But the same degree sequence is not *sufficient* — there exist non-isomorphic graphs with identical degree sequences (a topic for further exploration).

> **Side Note:** Determining whether two graphs are isomorphic is a famous open problem in computer science. The **graph isomorphism problem** is not known to be solvable in polynomial time, nor is it known to be NP-complete. It sits in a rare middle ground — one of very few natural problems with this ambiguous complexity status.

## Key Takeaways

<LectureNotes>

**Graph Basics:**

- A graph $G = (V, E)$ consists of vertices (nodes) and edges (connections)
- **Undirected** edges are sets <InlineMath formula="\{u, v\}" />; **directed** edges are tuples $(u, v)$
- **Degree** $\deg(v)$ counts edges touching $v$; directed graphs split into in-degree and out-degree
- **Order** $= |V|$, **Size** $= |E|$

**Walks, Trails, and Paths:**

- **Walk**: any vertex-edge sequence (repeats allowed)
- **Trail**: no repeated edges (but vertices may repeat)
- **Path**: no repeated vertices

**Euler's Theorem:**

- **Euler circuit** exists iff every vertex has even degree
- **Euler trail** (not circuit) exists iff exactly 2 vertices have odd degree
- Königsberg: all 4 vertices odd → impossible. Modern Kaliningrad: 2 odd → possible

**Representations:**

- **Adjacency matrix**: $O(1)$ edge lookup, $O(n^2)$ space, symmetric for undirected
- **Adjacency list**: $O(n + m)$ space, $O(\deg)$ lookup — `dict[str, set[str]]` in Python
- **Edge set**: formal definition as set of `frozenset` (undirected) or `tuple` (directed)

**Isomorphisms:**

- Two graphs are isomorphic if a bijection $f: V_1 \to V_2$ preserves adjacency
- Weaker than equality — same structure, possibly different labels
- Same degree sequence is necessary but not sufficient
- The graph isomorphism problem has unknown complexity (neither known to be in P nor NP-complete)
