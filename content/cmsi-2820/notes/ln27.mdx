## Artificial Intelligence

The AI elective group offers **CMSI 3300 (Artificial Intelligence)**, **CMSI 4320 (Cognitive Systems Design)**, **CMSI 5350 (Machine Learning)**, and **CMSI 5370 (Natural Language Processing)**. Every CS major must take at least one.

### Game Trees and Minimax — a historical detour

Before we dive into the AI courses, a brief detour. The minimax algorithm below — searching a game tree, propagating min/max scores from leaves upward — *used* to be one of the canonical "AI" topics. Today it has trickled down into our **algorithms** course (CMSI 2130), because as the field of AI advances, yesterday's frontier becomes tomorrow's textbook chapter. It still belongs in this lecture as the bridge: it is purely **graph theory** (LN20–23) — the game tree is a rooted tree, each node is a state, each edge is a legal move, and the AI does a depth-limited DFS evaluating positions:

<GameTreeExplorer
  initialBoard={["X", "O", "", "", "X", "", "", "", "O"]}
  maxDepth={3}
/>

Click on child boards to explore different move sequences. Watch how the minimax scores propagate upward — X (the maximizer) picks the child with the highest score; O (the minimizer) picks the child with the lowest. The tree grows exponentially with depth — that combinatorial explosion (LN17) is what makes AlphaZero's neural network position evaluator necessary, since you can never search the whole tree for chess or Go.

### CMSI 3300 — Bayesian Networks and Inference

CMSI 3300 is **classical AI**: the topics that defined the field for its first 50 years. Among the threads you'll meet are search algorithms, knowledge representation in formal logic (LN1–3), Markov chains (sequences of states with transition probabilities — LN20 weighted directed graphs!), particle filters for tracking objects from noisy sensors, and toward the end the **linear perceptron** that seeded the neural-network thread.

But the centerpiece for many students is **Bayesian networks** — directed acyclic graphs (LN20) where nodes are random variables and edges encode probabilistic dependencies. Click any node in the canonical *burglary / earthquake / alarm / witness calls* network to set evidence and watch the posterior probabilities update via **enumeration inference**:

<BayesianNetworkExplorer />

The only inputs to the inference engine are: (1) the DAG structure (LN20), (2) the prior probabilities, and (3) the conditional probability tables. From those, sum over all <InlineMath formula="2^5 = 32" /> possible worlds, weight each by its joint probability, and renormalize. That is the entire algorithm. The set of worlds is a power set (LN10); the joint factorizes along the DAG (LN20); the renormalization is conditional probability (LN17). Pure discrete math, from the ground up.

> **Key Insight:** Bayesian networks reformulate "uncertain reasoning" as *graph theory + arithmetic*. The graph structure (LN20) tells you which variables can influence which; the math is just multiplication and addition.

### CMSI 4320 — Reinforcement Learning and Bandits

CMSI 4320 (Cognitive Systems Design) builds directly on 3300. Topics include **exact, approximate, and deep Q-learning**, alongside the elegant **multi-armed bandit problem** that captures the explore-vs-exploit dilemma in its purest form. You walk into a casino with N slot machines, each with a hidden payout rate. How should you spend your pulls?

<MultiArmedBanditPlayground />

Switch between strategies and watch the regret curves diverge. **Pure greedy** locks onto the first arm that pays out and may never discover a better one. **ε-greedy** keeps a small probability of random exploration. **UCB** (upper confidence bound) explicitly favors arms it's more uncertain about. The math behind UCB is direct from LN17 combinatorics — a confidence bound from concentration inequalities.

Once you can solve bandits, you scale up to full reinforcement learning. **Q-learning** generalizes the bandit problem to a sequence of states connected by actions — a directed graph (LN20). Each state-action pair has a learned value <InlineMath formula="Q(s, a)" />, updated with the famous Bellman backup:

<DisplayMath formula="Q(s, a) \leftarrow Q(s, a) + \alpha \big[ r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s', a') - Q(s, a) \big]" />

Train a Q-table on a tiny gridworld and watch the policy crystallize:

<QLearningGrid />

Click *+50 episodes* a few times. The cells next to the pit (✕) stay red; a green corridor of high values forms from the start (S) to the goal (★); the arrows show the greedy policy. Once you can build the Q-table for a small grid, you replace the table with a neural network and you have **deep Q-learning** — the algorithm that beat humans at Atari games in 2013 and powers many real-world recommender and control systems today.

### CMSI 5350 / 5370 — Machine Learning, NLP, and the agentic moment

CMSI 5350 (Machine Learning) and CMSI 5370 (Natural Language Processing) are the graduate-level continuations of the AI thread. I haven't studied either as closely as the courses above, so I'll keep this section short and let the visual carry the weight.

The single most important idea in modern ML is **gradient descent**: pick any differentiable loss function, take its derivative (the *gradient*), and nudge your parameters slightly in the direction that decreases the loss. Repeat. Drag the start point and step size on the 1-D landscape below to feel it:

<GradientDescentDemo />

Try a tiny step size (slow but steady), then a huge one (overshoots, oscillates, may diverge). That single principle — *nudge against the gradient* — is what trains every neural network on Earth, including the **LLMs behind tools like Cursor, GitHub Copilot, and ChatGPT**. Modern *agentic coding tools* — the AI assistants that write, run, and debug code alongside a developer — are direct applications of the transformer architecture, gradient descent at scale (with billions of parameters), and reinforcement-learning fine-tuning (RLHF — exactly the Q-learning lineage from above). Discrete math (LN10 sets of tokens, LN15 functions, LN17 combinatorics of attention) is the underlying language; calculus is the optimizer. They are not separate worlds.

> **Key Insight:** From the perceptron in CMSI 3300 to the transformers in CMSI 5370, the math is the same recipe — define a loss, take the gradient, descend. The differences are in scale, in architecture, and in the cleverness of the loss function.

## Game Development

The Games and Interaction group offers **CMSI 3700 (Interaction Architecture)**, **CMSI 3710 (Computer Graphics)**, **CMSI 3751 (Game Design)**, and **CMSI 3752 (Game Development)**. Two of these — CMSI 3700 and 3710 — can also satisfy university core requirements, freeing up elective space.

### CMSI 3700 — Interaction Architecture and A/B Testing

CMSI 3700 is the UI/UX fundamentals course: how do you design an interface humans actually want to use, and how do you *measure* whether you got it right? The single most-used measurement tool in industry is the **A/B test**: split your users randomly, show variant A to half and variant B to the other, count conversions, and run a hypothesis test to see if the difference is real or just noise.

<ABTestSimulator />

Try setting both true rates to **the same value** and clicking auto-stream. The "winner" will flip between A and B for thousands of visitors before settling. That is exactly what false-positive bias looks like in real product decisions — and is the entire reason why the math (a two-proportion z-test, built from LN17 combinatorics and a normal-CDF calculation) matters more than the gut. CMSI 3700 teaches you to design experiments that resist this trap.

### CMSI 3710 — Computer Graphics from Scratch

CMSI 3710 is one of the most beloved courses in the curriculum. A central project is building a **3D rendering engine in raw GLSL** rather than leaning on a pre-built engine. Along the way you hand-roll the matrix operations that drive lighting, transformations, and camera projections, and explore pixel filters and basic image compression as fragment shaders.

The rendering pipeline is, almost end to end, **linear algebra over matrices**. Every vertex of every triangle of every model gets multiplied by a chain of 4×4 matrices on its way to your screen — model → world → view → projection → viewport. Here is the 2D analog so you can feel the composition order:

<MatrixTransformPlayground />

Slide *rotate*, watch the green triangle spin. Slide *shear* — see the parallelogram. Composition is **right-to-left**: the rightmost matrix in `M = T·R·K·S` is applied first to the input vertex. CMSI 3710 makes you internalize this so deeply that you can derive *why* rotating around an arbitrary point requires a translate-rotate-translate sandwich.

The other half of the rendering pipeline is **per-pixel** work — convolution kernels for blur, sharpen, edge detection, embossing, and ultimately the building blocks of compression schemes like JPEG. Hover any pixel below to see the exact 9-neighbor sum that produces the output:

<PixelFilterDemo />

The kernel is a tiny 3×3 matrix; the operation is the **discrete convolution** of the kernel with the image. The same computation appears in JPEG's discrete cosine transform, in the convolutional layers of every image-recognition neural network, and in the compute shaders of every modern game's post-processing pipeline. Matrix math (LN15 functions over a 2-D grid, LN17 combinatorics of the kernel sweep) all the way down.

### CMSI 3751 — Game Design and Combinatorics

CMSI 3751 is the **hands-on physical design** course — board games, card games, dice games, and other tabletop systems. The emphasis is on rules, balance, and player experience rather than code, building the intuition you'd need to design something like *Magic: The Gathering* or *Settlers of Catan*.

The math you reach for constantly is the **binomial coefficient** from LN16–17. How likely is a player to draw the right opening hand? That single question — known formally as the *hypergeometric distribution* — drives deck-building rules across nearly every collectible card game:

<DeckCombinatoricsExplorer />

Slide the deck size up to 60, hand size to 7, specials to 4 — that's roughly an *MTG* mulligan calculation. Game designers tune these three knobs (deck size, hand size, rare-card density) to hit a target opening-hand variance. Get it wrong and your game feels swingy or boring. The defense of those numbers in a CMSI 3751 design doc *is* a binomial-coefficient argument.

### CMSI 3510 — Operating Systems

In **CMSI 3510 (Operating Systems)** — which I personally teach — you encounter problems like CPU scheduling, memory management, and deadlock detection, all of which have direct graph-theoretic formulations:

| OS Problem | Graph Formulation | LN Reference |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Process scheduling | Graph coloring (time slots = colors) | LN23: chromatic number |
| Deadlock detection | Cycle detection in directed resource graph | LN20: walks and cycles |
| File system hierarchy | Tree traversal | LN23: trees |
| Page tables | Multi-level tree structure | LN23: trees |
| Network topology | Specific graph families (hypercubes, meshes) | LN22: graph families |
| Load balancing | Pigeonhole principle (distribute evenly) | LN19: pigeonhole |

Deadlock is the single best example. Processes (circles) hold resources (squares) and request others; when those dependencies form a *cycle* in the resource allocation graph, no process can proceed. Drag in dependencies until you trigger the cycle:

<DeadlockExplorer />

This is exactly the **cycle detection** problem from LN20 (walks, cycles) and exactly the kind of structural reasoning that CMSI 3510 teaches you to do at scale. Want the full lecture treatment — the four Coffman conditions, resource-allocation graph algorithms, dining philosophers, and the Banker's Algorithm? See my own course's [LN 12: Don't Starve](/cmsi-3510/ln12) for the deep dive.

> **Side Note:** Cisco's **Spanning Tree Protocol (STP)** — which prevents loops in Ethernet networks — literally computes a spanning tree (LN23) of the network graph. The protocol name IS the graph theory concept.

### CMSI 3550 — Networks and Internets

When you send a packet across the internet, it doesn't just hop randomly — it traverses a deeply hierarchical graph. Your phone or laptop sits at the **edge**. Your home router and ISP form the next tier up. At the top sit the **backbone** providers and Internet Exchange Points (IXPs) that connect ISPs to each other across continents. Almost every cross-network packet route looks the same shape: *up* through the hierarchy to the backbone, *across*, then back *down*:

<InternetTopologyMap />

Pick any source and destination from the dropdowns and animate the packet. Notice how routes between two edge devices on different ISPs always pull the packet up to the backbone before they can come back down — that is the *hierarchical* structure that real internet routing protocols exploit. **OSPF** (Open Shortest Path First) runs Dijkstra (LN23) *within* a single ISP's network. **BGP** (Border Gateway Protocol) does shortest-path-with-policy routing *between* ISPs at the backbone tier. Every weighted edge in the visualization above models real-world latency; the math that turns those edges into routes is the same Dijkstra you saw in LN23.

> **Key Insight:** The internet is not a single graph; it is a *graph of graphs*, and the hierarchy is what makes routing tractable at planetary scale. CMSI 3550 teaches you the protocols that maintain this structure — and how to break it (denial of service, BGP hijacks, censorship circumvention).

### Research Frontiers

Discrete mathematics powers cutting-edge research across many fields. A few you might want to chase:

- **Quantum machine learning (VQE — Variational Quantum Eigensolver).** A hybrid quantum/classical algorithm: a parameterized quantum circuit prepares a candidate state, a classical computer measures the energy, and **gradient descent** (yes, that gradient descent) updates the parameters. Boolean algebra from LN5 generalizes from {0, 1} to qubit superpositions; the optimization is identical to the ML you saw above.
- **AlphaFold and computational biology.** AlphaFold predicts protein structures by treating the residue chain as a **graph** (LN20) where edges are predicted spatial contacts. Attention weights become edge probabilities; folding the protein becomes shortest-path-style reasoning over that contact graph. Computational biology was the original use case for **Euler paths** (LN20) in genome assembly via de Bruijn graphs.
- **Rust's type system.** The Rust borrow checker is an honest-to-God formal proof system: it statically rejects programs that would have memory bugs by treating ownership as a *linear* (or *affine*) type — drawing directly on the lambda calculus and type theory threads from LN12–14 and LN26's [CMSI 5850 vignette](/cmsi-2820/ln26#programming-languages-foundations--cmsi-5850-graduate). Rust is the first mainstream language to commercialize results that lived in PL research papers for 30+ years.
- **Post-quantum cryptography (lattice-based schemes).** Shor's algorithm breaks RSA on a sufficiently powerful quantum computer. NIST's recently standardized replacements (Kyber, Dilithium) base their hardness on **lattice problems** — combinatorial puzzles in <InlineMath formula="\mathbb{Z}^n" /> that no efficient quantum algorithm is known to solve. Modular arithmetic (LN7) again, just over much higher-dimensional spaces.

## The Final Pattern

In LN24, we revealed the hidden curriculum of this course: every topic follows **Type → Instances → Operators**. Now, at the end of our tour, notice that the same pattern applies to every field we visited:

| Field | Type | Instances | Operators |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Bayesian inference | Probability distributions | Specific posteriors | Conditioning, marginalization |
| Reinforcement learning | State-action value tables | Q-functions | Bellman update |
| Modern ML | Differentiable functions | Trained models | Gradient descent |
| Graphics | Affine transforms | Specific matrices | Composition (right-to-left) |
| A/B testing | Hypotheses | Sample proportions | z-test, p-value |

The meta-pattern holds. It will continue to hold in every course you take, every language you learn, and every problem you solve.

### Historical Context

- **John von Neumann (1903–1957)** — Co-founded game theory with the minimax theorem (1928); the same von Neumann who gave us the ordinal construction in LN24
- **Peter Hart, Nils Nilsson, Bertram Raphael** — Invented A* at Stanford Research Institute (1968); still the gold standard for pathfinding 50+ years later
- **Claude Shannon (1916–2001)** — Proposed the first chess-playing algorithm (1950), building on his Boolean algebra work from LN26
- **Judea Pearl (b. 1936)** — Invented Bayesian networks and the modern theory of causal inference; Turing Award 2011
- **Richard Bellman (1920–1984)** — Coined "dynamic programming" and the Bellman equation that powers Q-learning
- **Frank Rosenblatt (1928–1971)** — Built the original perceptron (1957), the seed that grew into modern deep learning
- **Geoffrey Hinton, Yann LeCun, Yoshua Bengio** — Turing Award 2018 for deep learning; their backprop paper relies entirely on gradient descent
- **Radia Perlman (b. 1951)** — Invented the Spanning Tree Protocol (STP) for Ethernet networks, directly implementing the graph theory spanning tree concept
- **Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn** — Designed TCP/IP (1974), the protocol stack underpinning the entire hierarchy in the Internet Topology Map above

- `glm` and `gl-matrix` are the production-grade matrix libraries CMSI 3710 might let you use after you've written your own
- Game engines (Unity, Unreal, Godot) implement pathfinding using navigation meshes — graph representations of walkable space
